background

Currently, high throughput (HT) technologies (where ten of thousands of samples undergo simultaneous testing for measurements of gene expressions levels) output such massive volumes of data, researchers are left with the rather daunting task of finding if there are any meaningful patterns in the data at all.

A whole host of datasets, resources and tools are at your disposal and imagine you want to use these to answer your research questions, for example:

  • Is there a difference between two populations as opposed to none?
  • Why does this difference exist?
  • Can it also implicate something else?
  • Is this always the case?
  • What are ways we can measure this difference?

A crucial first step is to identify what your specific research goal is and then plan your experiment accordingly. If you’re interested in studying the functional aspect of genes and what role they might have in a given biological process or phenotype, pathway analysis can be the way to go.

Earlier, one could compare two groups by looking at a single gene at a time (e.g., t-test) and determine which genes are statistically significant differentially expressed and which ones are not. But this approach tended to lack context; what if the cumulative effect of a large number of genes that change is greater than the effect of a single one? How would we know that? Instead of looking at individual genes, we could consider them in sets of related ones, in other words, conduct a pathway analysis [Khatri].

data

Once you have a goal in mind, choosing the appropriate HT technology and defining a good experimental design are crucial.

Types of technologies to use can include microarray and RNA-sequencing.

A common aim of both these technologies is gene expression (GE) profiling to determine what patterns of genes are expressed at the transcription level given a certain condition.

In other words, what is the relative level of mRNA given a specific condition? Or, which condition resulted in a specific gene being expressed?

One of the limitations of using microarray data is that it requires prior knowledge of the genomic sequence. In contrast, RNA-sequencing (RNA-seq) doesn’t require this prior knowledge, is associated with decreasing costs, has greater sensitivity for genes expressed at both very high and very low levels and for this and several other advantages, has become a popular tool for gene expression studies.

You can check out these great resources for more details: https://github.com/saezlab/transcriptutorial
